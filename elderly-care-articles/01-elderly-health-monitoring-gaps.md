## Introduction

A quarterly doctor visit captures about 0.1% of a senior's life. The other 99.9%—where health changes actually happen—remains invisible. This monitoring gap explains why so many health crises in the elderly "come out of nowhere."

---

## The Snapshot Problem

### What Check-Ups Capture

| Visit Component | Time | Health Captured |
|----------------|------|-----------------|
| Vital signs | 5 minutes | That moment only |
| Physical exam | 10 minutes | Visible issues |
| Patient report | 10 minutes | What's remembered |
| Lab work | Delayed results | Static markers |

### What's Missed

| Health Change | Visibility |
|---------------|------------|
| Gradual vital sign changes | Invisible between visits |
| Sleep quality decline | Not assessed |
| Activity level drops | Patient may not notice |
| Heart rhythm variations | Brief check misses |
| Recovery capacity changes | Never measured |

---

## Why Periodic Monitoring Fails

### The Math Problem

| Monitoring Type | Coverage |
|-----------------|----------|
| Quarterly visit | 0.1% of time |
| Monthly visit | 0.4% of time |
| Weekly home health | 1.4% of time |
| Continuous wearable | 100% of time |

### The Baseline Problem

| Issue | Consequence |
|-------|-------------|
| No personal baseline | Can't detect "different for you" |
| Population norms only | Individual variation ignored |
| Symptom-based triggers | Catch problems too late |
| Memory-based history | Inaccurate recall |
