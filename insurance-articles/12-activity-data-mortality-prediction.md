## Introduction

The average American takes 3,000-4,000 steps per day. The difference between taking 4,000 steps and 10,000 steps predicts mortality risk better than many factors actuaries have relied on for decades.

Activity data from wearables isn't just a wellness metric—it's a powerful predictor of longevity. Research consistently shows that daily movement patterns, intensity distribution, and activity trends correlate strongly with all-cause mortality.

**This article examines the evidence connecting activity data to mortality prediction and its implications for life insurance.**

---

## The Research Foundation

### Key Studies

**1. JAMA Internal Medicine (2019):**
- 16,741 women with wearable activity data
- 4-year follow-up
- Those with 4,400 steps/day had 41% lower mortality vs. 2,700 steps/day
- Benefits plateaued around 7,500 steps/day

**2. UK Biobank Analysis (2022):**
- 78,500 participants with accelerometer data
- 7-year follow-up
- Optimal step count: 9,000-10,000 for adults under 60
- 6,000-8,000 for adults over 60
- Each 2,000-step increase reduced mortality 8-11%

**3. Meta-Analysis (2020):**
- Combined data from 15 studies
- 47,471 participants
- Strong dose-response relationship between steps and mortality
- Intensity mattered: vigorous steps more protective

### The Mortality Gradient

| Daily Steps | Relative Mortality Risk | Risk Classification |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| < 4,000 | 1.0 (reference) | High risk |
| 4,000-6,000 | 0.60 | Moderate risk |
| 6,000-8,000 | 0.45 | Standard |
| 8,000-10,000 | 0.35 | Preferred |
| > 10,000 | 0.30 | Super-preferred |

**Key finding:** Low activity is as powerful a mortality predictor as smoking status.

## Why Activity Predicts Mortality

### The Biological Mechanisms

**Physical activity affects multiple mortality pathways:**

| System | Activity Effect | Mortality Impact |
|--------|----------------|------------------|
| Cardiovascular | Lower BP, improved lipids | CVD death reduction |
| Metabolic | Better insulin sensitivity | Diabetes prevention |
| Immune | Enhanced function | Lower infection mortality |
| Musculoskeletal | Preserved function | Fall prevention |
| Cognitive | Brain health | Dementia reduction |
| Mental health | Mood improvement | Suicide prevention |

### Activity as an Integrated Health Marker

Activity levels reflect:
- Overall health status
- Functional capacity
- Energy and vitality
- Absence of debilitating conditions
- Health-conscious behavior

**Low activity often precedes other health problems**, making it a leading indicator of decline.

## Activity Metrics for Underwriting

### Daily Step Count

**The simplest and most researched metric:**

| Metric | Value | Assessment |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Average daily steps | < 4,000 | High risk |
| Average daily steps | 4,000-7,000 | Standard |
| Average daily steps | 7,000-10,000 | Preferred |
| Average daily steps | > 10,000 | Super-preferred |

### Activity Intensity Distribution

**Beyond steps—intensity matters:**

| Intensity | Definition | Mortality Impact |
|-----------|------------|------------------|
| Sedentary | < 100 steps/min | Harmful |
| Light | 100-130 steps/min | Minimal benefit |
| Moderate | 130-150 steps/min | Significant benefit |
| Vigorous | > 150 steps/min | Maximum benefit |

**Recommendation:** Track moderate-to-vigorous physical activity (MVPA) minutes, not just steps.

### Activity Trends

**Direction matters as much as absolute level:**

| Trend | Interpretation | Underwriting Impact |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| Stable high | Consistent healthy lifestyle | Favorable |
| Improving | Positive behavior change | Favorable |
| Stable low | Chronic inactivity | Unfavorable |
| Declining | Health deterioration | Red flag |

**Declining activity often precedes health events by 6-12 months.**

## Sedentary Behavior: The Hidden Risk

### Sitting Is the New Smoking

**Research findings:**
- Sitting > 8 hours/day increases mortality 20-40%
- Independent of exercise (can't "exercise away" sitting)
- Breaks in sitting reduce risk
- Standing and light movement matter

### Sedentary Metrics

| Metric | Risk Threshold |
|--------|---------------|
| Daily sitting time | > 8 hours high risk |
| Continuous sitting bouts | > 60 minutes without break |
| Screen time | > 4 hours associated with higher mortality |
| Sleep plus sitting | > 16 hours combined |

### The Activity Paradox

**Some people exercise but remain sedentary:**
- 45-minute gym session
- 8+ hours sitting at desk
- 4 hours sitting in evening

**Total activity matters**, not just intentional exercise.

## Age-Specific Considerations

### Younger Adults (18-40)

**Patterns to assess:**
- Baseline activity level
- Weekend vs. weekday patterns
- Sports and recreational activity
- Active transportation

**Note:** Lower correlation with near-term mortality, but strong predictor of healthy aging trajectory.

### Middle Age (40-60)

**Critical period for assessment:**
- Activity often declines with career demands
- Family responsibilities affect patterns
- Early indicators of chronic disease
- Intervention most effective here

**Key metrics:** Decline from earlier activity levels, recovery capacity.

### Older Adults (60+)

**Strongest mortality prediction:**
- Even modest activity highly protective
- Functional capacity indicators
- Balance between activity and safety
- Consistency matters more than intensity

**Key metrics:** Ability to maintain activity, walking speed, daily consistency.

## Wearable Data Advantages

### Continuous vs. Self-Report

**Self-reported activity problems:**
- Overestimation (30-50% common)
- Social desirability bias
- Recall inaccuracy
- Snapshot rather than pattern

**Wearable advantages:**
- Objective measurement
- Continuous tracking
- Pattern recognition
- Trend detection

### What Wearables Capture

| Data Type | Underwriting Value |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Step count | Primary activity metric |
| Active minutes | Intensity assessment |
| Sedentary time | Inactivity risk |
| Sleep movement | Health indicator |
| Workout frequency | Intentional exercise |
| Hourly movement | Pattern consistency |

### Data Quality Considerations

**Ensuring reliable data:**
- Wear time requirements (12+ hours/day)
- Minimum observation period (30+ days)
- Accounting for device removal
- Seasonal adjustment if needed

## Implementation for Life Insurance

### Underwriting Integration

**Workflow:**

1. **Data collection:** 30-90 days of wearable activity data
2. **Quality screening:** Sufficient wear time and observation
3. **Metric calculation:** Steps, intensity, sedentary time, trends
4. **Risk scoring:** Activity-based mortality adjustment
5. **Integration:** Combine with traditional factors
6. **Continuous monitoring:** Post-issue tracking (if consented)

### Pricing Adjustments

**Sample activity-based rate modification:**

| Activity Profile | Rate Adjustment |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| High activity, consistent | 15-20% discount |
| Moderate activity | Standard |
| Low activity, stable | 10-15% loading |
| Declining activity | Individual assessment |

### Accelerated Underwriting

**Activity data enables:**
- Faster decisions (objective data vs. exams)
- Risk stratification without fluids
- Continuous verification of lifestyle claims
- Post-issue monitoring programs

## Case Study: Activity-Based Underwriting

### Traditional Assessment

- 45-year-old male
- BMI 28 (overweight)
- No diagnosed conditions
- Self-reports "occasional exercise"
- Traditional underwriting: Standard rate

### With Activity Data

- Daily steps: 11,500 average
- Moderate activity: 45+ minutes/day
- Weekend activity: Higher than weekday
- Trend: Stable over 6 months
- Sedentary time: Low (< 6 hours/day)

### Revised Assessment

- Activity profile: Excellent
- Fitness level: High despite BMI
- Mortality risk: Significantly lower than BMI suggests
- Revised rating: Preferred

**Outcome:** Activity data revealed hidden health; pricing improved for both insurer and customer.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Steps predict mortality:** Research confirms strong correlation
2. **Dose-response relationship:** More activity = lower mortality up to ~10,000 steps
3. **Low activity = high risk:** As predictive as smoking for mortality
4. **Intensity matters:** Moderate-vigorous activity more protective
5. **Sedentary behavior:** Independent risk factor beyond exercise
6. **Trends reveal trajectory:** Declining activity is a red flag
7. **Age-adjusted assessment:** Different thresholds for different ages
8. **Wearable data superior:** Objective, continuous, pattern-detecting
